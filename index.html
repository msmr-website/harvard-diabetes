Why Diabetes?

Type 1 diabetes, also known as insulin-dependent diabetes, is a disease where the immune system, responsible for detecting and attacking harmful pathogens, destroys insulin-producing cell clusters in the pancreas. Since it is a disease where the immune system ends up attacking itself, it is known as an autoimmune disease.

These pancreatic clusters are known as islets. Each islet consists of several types of cells, which work together to regulate blood sugar, but the most significant one for this breakthrough is the beta cell. Beta cells respond to glucose in the blood and release the necessary amount of insulin that the body needs to maintain normal blood sugar levels.

Insulin serves as a key to open the cell and allow glucose to enter the cell. Without glucose, the glucose is forced to build up outside the cell. This is problematic for two main reasons. First, the body cells are essentially starved of glucose and can no longer derive the energy and resources they need from the glucose. Second, blood sugar builds up outside the cell, leading to a myriad of issues including eye, kidney, heart, and nerve damage.

Overview

In what is being hailed as a “giant leap against diabetes,” researchers from Harvard University have successfully created billions of glucose-sensing, insulin-producing beta cells using embryonic stem cells. By using stem cell differentiation to generate vast quantities of these mature beta cells, they believe they are at brink of developing a cure for type 1 diabetes, a disease that plagues nearly 30 million Americans.

According to an article published to Cell, this discovery is the most successful beta cell creation development to date, since the stem cells yield beta cells that closely resemble normal functioning beta cells in the large amounts necessary for cell transfer and pharmaceutical implementation.lls, they believe they are at brink of developing a cure for type 1 diabetes, a disease that plagues nearly 30 million Americans.

Doug Melton, Harvard professor and head researcher, is working towards human transplantation which he predicts will be viable within a few years. Melton’s work marks a milestone in his past twenty years spent in searching for a cure for this condition that afflicts both his children. The breakthrough, by attacking the very cause of type 1 diabetes issues, has enormous potential in future drugs and cells therapies to treat diabetes.

In 2004, Melton and his team dove into stem cell research with the creation of the Harvard Stem Cell Institute and the goal of transforming stem cells into beta cells, which produce insulin. Stem cells are undifferentiated cells that can differentiate into specialized cells and can divide by mitosis to produce more stem cells. Human embryonic stem cells are made of cells derived from embryos, while induced pluripotent cells are adult cells that have been genetically reprogrammed to an embryonic stem cell–like state. Although past research has been able to develop stem cells into beta cells, these beta cells have not been mature enough to respond to glucose and mark themselves as beta cells, two critical characteristics for insulin-production by beta cells.

Melton’s team grew three stem cell lines - one human embryonic stem cell line and two human-induced pluripotent stem cell lines - allowing them to produce a vast number of stem cells for testing. In the past, scientists have used small proteins or molecules to drive the development of the beta cells. Unfortunately, while the beta cell might develop, these small molecules or proteins mix with other proteins and often activate or inhibit certain signals, therefore causing the gene expression to be altered. Since the cell differentiates and redefines its function, it is difficult to determine the correct factors to get the correct cell. This correct combination, which would take researchers from the pancreatic progenitor to a mature beta cell, had not been discovered. After testing over 150 combinations of over 70 compounds, Melton and his team found 11 different factors on 5 different growth mediums that that pushed them to the discovery of the functional beta cell. Using this winning combination, they applied it several times and fully transformed the adult pluripotent stem cells into insulin-producing beta cells.

The beta cells were a success. The cultured cells didn’t have any of the issues found in previous studies. They had the markers found on normal beta cells and responded to glucose with calcium change. Finally, truly marking the success of the research were the results after transplanting the new beta cells into mice. When the cells were transplanted into mice, they secreted insulin in response to glucose, and in diabetic mice, abnormally high blood glucose levels lowered.

Conclusion

"We are now just one pre-clinical step away from the finish line," said lead researcher and professor Doug Melton. The pre-clinical step he is referring to is stopping the immune system from destroying the newly made alien beta cells. After the scientific world is able to surpass this one last barrier, a cure for diabetes may be on the horizon. While diabetics can keep their glucose levels under general control via several insulin injections every day, this method is a crude, imprecise way of controlling metabolism. This lack of precision in insulin can yield devastating results, including loss of limb function and blindness, and with Melton’s discovery, these can be prevented. Furthermore, by serving as a cell source for islet replacement, this breakthrough opens the doors for a researchers to test and learn more about novel cell therapies for type 1 diabetes treatment. With Melton and his team’s breakthrough, our generation and generations to come will likely live better, healthier lives free of diabetes.
